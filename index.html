Confirmed cases vs. estimates of actual infections

Confirmed cases are only a (small and biased) subsample of actual infections. How small and how biased? It depends on the testing resources and protocols applied in each country/region.

Moreover the number of confirmed and notified deaths is also an estimate or actual deaths caused by the virus. And specially in some countries/regions this value could be underestimated. Statistics on mortality monitoring on all causes, as project EuroMomo does, can be useful to infer alternative (and better) estimates based on excess mortality. But it seems that COVID-19 virus (SARS-CoV-2) could be less lethal in relative terms than current figures we get in Italy or Spain (confirmed deaths/confirmed cases vs estimated deaths/estimated infections). Though much more contagious than we expected. So much more lethal in absolute terms. With the aggravating circumstance of saturating healthcare systems and increasing even more the number of direct and indirect deaths caused. Any confirmed cases and alternative kind of estimates of actual infections and deaths should be understood under their context. Language should be used rigourously to avoid misinformation.

Analysis: Distribution of Confirmed Cases by Age Groups

Spain vs Germany

The number of confirmed cases in Germany at 2 April is higher than 84K people, not so far from 112K confirmed cases in Spain (Johns Hopkins University, worldometer). But relative lethality estimates by COVID-19 on Germany seems 1%. Much lower than figures in Spain (9%) or Italy (12%).

There are several aspects than could explain this big difference. But I will focus on the representativity of the sample of confirmed tests in relation to the overall population. If we compare the distribution of confirmed cases by age in Spain and Germany we can see that:

  • In Spain confirmed cases are biased to ranges of older people. While young people would be underrepresented.
  • In Germany we have just the opposite effect. Older people is underrepresented.
We could think in South Korea as a country with a high number of tests performed and a more representative sample in terms of age distribution.

Thanks to datadista, Robert Koch Institute and plotly.
